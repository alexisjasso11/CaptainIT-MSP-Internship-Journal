## What I Did

### Synology NAS — Microsoft 365 Backup Configuration
> Continued building on NAS experience from Day 7 by configuring it to back up a cloud email account.

- Configured the Synology NAS to back up a **Microsoft 365 account**
- Ran into an issue where the NAS had no internet connection
- My boss helped identify the root cause: the **DNS server was set to one that was no longer active**
- Corrected the DNS configuration, resolving the connectivity issue
- Noted this as a learning moment — DNS settings are a common thing to overlook when troubleshooting connectivity, even though it wasn't the first thing I checked

### Customer Follow-Up Calls
> Followed up with customers originally contacted on Day 7 regarding a management software download.

- Called back the customers to confirm their management software installation
- Discovered that **no new PCs had joined the monitoring software** as expected
- Root cause: domain-joined accounts required **admin credentials** to complete the software download/installation
- Documented the issue for follow-up resolution

### On-Site Visit — Hard Drive Troubleshooting
> Tagged along with a coworker on-site to troubleshoot a hard drive that appeared non-functional.

- Assisted in diagnosing a hard drive reported as not working, with the intention of formatting it
- Found the root cause: the hard drive **simply was not powered on**
- Resolved the issue by ensuring proper power connection

---

## What I Learned
DNS misconfigurations can cause connectivity issues that look like a completely different problem at first glance — a good reminder to check basic network settings (DNS, IP config) early in troubleshooting rather than assuming hardware or software is at fault. On the customer side, I learned that domain-joined machines often require admin-level credentials for software installs, which is an important detail to confirm with customers ahead of time. And the hard drive issue was a great reminder that the simplest explanation — power — should always be checked first before assuming a more complex hardware failure.
